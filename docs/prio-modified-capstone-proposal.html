Introduction

Service-oriented establishments such as clinics, school offices, retail counters, and small business service desks often struggle with long physical lines, manual ticket handling, and poor visibility into queue progress. Customers are forced to remain near the service area because they do not know when they will be called, while staff must manually manage the order of service and respond repeatedly to follow-up questions. This problem leads to crowded waiting areas, missed turns, inconsistent service flow, and unnecessary operational pressure on front-desk personnel.

Existing digital queueing solutions already attempt to improve this situation. Commercial systems such as Qminder, QLESS, and similar queue management platforms provide digital check-ins, service dashboards, and customer notifications. Some organizations also use appointment systems, messaging-based queue updates, or standalone queue display systems to reduce physical crowding. These solutions demonstrate that digital queueing can improve customer experience, especially when it gives users visibility into queue progress and reduces idle waiting time.

Despite these existing solutions, several gaps remain for the context targeted by this project. Many available platforms are subscription-based, optimized for larger organizations, or designed as closed systems that are difficult to adapt for smaller local service providers. Other solutions focus only on check-in or only on appointment booking, but do not combine QR-based entry, live queue monitoring, tenant-specific management, and configurable near-turn notifications in one lightweight web application. There is also a need for a system that allows multiple independent vendors to operate under one platform while still keeping their queue settings and ticket records separated.

The proposed solution is Prio, a multi-tenant QR-based digital queue management platform designed for small and medium-sized service environments. Prio gives each vendor its own workspace, public queue monitor, queue settings, and QR-based join link while allowing customers to join remotely or on site. The system generates daily ticket numbers, provides live public queue updates, and supports email or SMS alerts when a customer is near the front or already called for service. By combining accessibility, tenant-aware design, and real-time queue visibility, the proposed platform directly addresses the identified gaps in existing queueing approaches.

Proposed Objectives

General Objective: To develop a multi-tenant digital queue management platform that improves service efficiency through QR-based joining, live queue monitoring, and near-turn customer notifications.

Specific Objectives:

  • Enable vendor registration and tenant-specific queue workspace creation.
  • Allow customers to join queues through QR codes or online join pages.
  • Generate daily ticket numbers and lookup codes for queue tracking.
  • Provide vendor controls for call next, serve current, skip, and walk-in issuance.
  • Display a live public queue board accessible through a browser.
  • Send email or SMS alerts when a customer is almost next or already called.

Proposed Features

Must-Have Features

  • Vendor onboarding and tenant creation
  • Customer account registration
  • QR-based queue entry
  • Online queue join page
  • Daily ticket generation with queue prefixes
  • Vendor dashboard for queue control
  • Live public queue monitor

Nice-to-Have Features

  • Multiple counters per tenant
  • Business hours and queue availability controls
  • Separate owner and staff roles
  • Queue analytics and reporting
  • Appointment windows or scheduled visits
  • Rate limiting and CAPTCHA for public ticket creation

Feasibility for Proposal Consultation

The proposed system is feasible within the scope of a capstone project because its core workflow is clear and measurable: a customer joins a queue, receives a ticket, monitors queue movement, and is eventually called for service through a vendor-controlled dashboard. The selected technology stack is appropriate for a BSIT web development capstone because it requires applied work in interface design, backend API development, database modeling, real-time event handling, and external service integration.

The project also has a clear deliverable that can be demonstrated during consultation and defense: a working web prototype that shows vendor onboarding, QR queue entry, real-time monitoring, and queue state transitions. This makes the project suitable for evaluation in terms of technical implementation, usability, and problem-solution alignment.
